# 2026 Web Research Update

## Purpose

This note summarizes public web research completed on April 9, 2026 to update the ISO 42001 study pack with current ecosystem developments, credible implementation guidance, and legitimate exam-prep observations.

## Key Standard Updates Around ISO 42001

### 1. ISO/IEC 42001 remains the core AIMS standard

Public ISO information confirms that ISO/IEC 42001:2023 is the main international standard for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving an Artificial Intelligence Management System.

What matters for study:

* It is still the central certifiable management system standard.
* It applies to organizations that develop, provide, or use AI-based products or services.
* Its value proposition remains responsible AI governance, risk and opportunity management, traceability, transparency, and continual improvement.

### 2. ISO/IEC 42005:2025 is now important supporting guidance

The official ISO 42001 product page now explicitly bundles ISO/IEC 42001 with ISO/IEC 42005:2025 as a responsible AI governance and impact standards package.

What matters for study:

* 42005 strengthens the importance of AI system impact assessment.
* Expect more attention on impact assessment methodology, affected people, and societal consequences.
* This reinforces that impact assessment is not a side topic; it is part of the broader ISO 42001 ecosystem.

### 3. ISO/IEC 42006:2025 is now part of the certification ecosystem

Public ISO and IEC references describe ISO/IEC 42006:2025 as the standard that adds requirements for bodies that audit and certify AIMS according to ISO/IEC 42001.

What matters for study:

* The certification market is maturing.
* Auditor competence, impartiality, and AI-specific certification capability are becoming more formalized.
* This does not change clause requirements for your exam directly, but it is a strong sign that ISO 42001 certification is moving from early adoption into a more structured assurance phase.

## Implementation Trends Seen in Public 2025 to 2026 Material

### 1. Enterprise adoption is accelerating

Recent public material indicates that ISO 42001 has moved quickly from a new standard into active enterprise certification and adoption conversations.

Repeated signals in public sources:

* Microsoft publicly documents ISO/IEC 42001 coverage for several Copilot-related services, including GitHub Copilot.
* Public implementation commentary highlights certifications or certification activity across large AI providers and regulated organizations.
* Training and certification providers now have full ISO 42001 learning tracks rather than only awareness-level content.

Practical implication:

* ISO 42001 is no longer only a conceptual framework. It is being operationalized in real service environments.

### 2. AI inventory and scope definition are becoming major implementation priorities

Recent implementation guidance repeatedly emphasizes that organizations struggle first with understanding what AI systems they actually have in scope.

Common themes:

* AI inventory comes early.
* Shadow AI is a major issue.
* Scope is often phased instead of enterprise-wide on day one.
* Organizations must identify whether they act as provider, customer, partner, or another role in the AI ecosystem.

Study implication:

* Expect exam questions that test scope logic, AI roles, and implementation sequencing.

### 3. Evidence collection is a real-world bottleneck

Recent guidance consistently says the hardest part is not policy writing but gathering operational evidence.

Examples mentioned in public material:

* Model cards
* Testing and validation logs
* Bias assessment records
* Data lineage and provenance records
* Change management logs
* Human oversight records
* Risk and impact assessment outputs

Study implication:

* Questions are likely to favor implementation evidence over theory-only answers.
* When unsure in the exam, think like an auditor: what document, record, or output proves this?

### 4. EU AI Act alignment is a major trend, but not equivalence

Recent implementation material strongly connects ISO 42001 with EU AI Act readiness, especially because of the approaching August 2026 high-risk obligations timeline referenced in public commentary.

Important nuance:

* ISO 42001 helps organizations prepare for regulatory compliance.
* ISO 42001 certification is not the same as automatic EU AI Act compliance.
* Public guidance highlights overlap in risk management, human oversight, transparency, data governance, and monitoring.

Study implication:

* Expect exam questions or discussions about regulatory alignment, but avoid overstating certification as legal equivalence.

## Question Trends in Public Prep Material

Based on public training pages, prep-provider snippets, and implementation guidance, the strongest recurring topic clusters are:

### 1. Clause 4 to 10 structure and PDCA logic

This remains the dominant backbone.

Likely focus areas:

* Context and interested parties
* Leadership responsibilities
* Planning and measurable objectives
* Support and documented information
* Operation and lifecycle execution
* Performance evaluation
* Corrective action and continual improvement

### 2. AI risk assessment versus AI system impact assessment

This appears repeatedly in both training material and implementation commentary.

Likely focus areas:

* Differences between the two concepts
* When each is used
* Why both matter
* How impact assessment considers broader consequences

### 3. Statement of Applicability and Annex A control logic

Public implementation guidance increasingly highlights:

* The SoA as a baseline document
* Annex A having 38 controls across nine domains
* Control selection and exclusion rationale
* The need to align controls with scope and risk

### 4. Top management and management system accountability

Recurring public emphasis includes:

* Top management responsibility for policy and review
* Leadership commitment
* Cross-functional governance
* Roles and responsibilities

### 5. Practical implementation sequencing

Recent materials heavily favor implementation order rather than isolated definitions.

Typical sequence:

* AI inventory
* Context and interested parties
* Scope
* Policy and roles
* Risk and impact assessments
* SoA and treatment planning
* Operational evidence
* Internal audit
* Management review
* Corrective action and continual improvement

## Sample Questions Versus Dumps

### What I found

Public search results contain three broad categories:

* Legitimate course pages and public certification-provider information
* Practice-question products from training platforms and commercial prep vendors
* Dump-style sites explicitly advertising real exam questions, verified dumps, or current exam content

### Recommendation

Do not rely on dump sites.

Reasons:

* They are ethically questionable.
* They are often inaccurate or low quality.
* They encourage memorization instead of management-system understanding.
* They create a risk of learning wrong terminology or wrong interpretation.

For your preparation, use only:

* Official ISO summaries and related standards references
* Official provider pages such as PECB for course scope and exam administration information
* Reputable explanatory guides for implementation context
* Your own transcript-based notes and practice questions

## What I Recommend Changing in Your Study Strategy

Based on the web research, the highest-value additions for exam preparation are:

* Spend extra time on AI system impact assessment.
* Strengthen understanding of Annex A and Statement of Applicability.
* Practice answering from an evidence and auditor perspective.
* Be ready to explain implementation order, not only clause definitions.
* Be careful not to overclaim that ISO 42001 certification equals regulatory compliance.

The 2026 direction is clear:
