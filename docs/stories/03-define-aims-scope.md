# User Story: 3 - Define AIMS Scope

**As a** AIMS implementation lead,
**I want** to define the scope of the AI management system,
**so that** the organization has a clear boundary for what is governed, audited, and certified.

## Acceptance Criteria

* The scope states the organizational boundaries, AI activities, and relevant systems covered by the AIMS.
* Scope exclusions are documented with justification.
* The scope reflects the documented context and interested party requirements.
* The approved scope is available for use in policy, risk, audit, and certification activities.

## Key Findings from Training (Day 1 & Day 3)

* The trainer was clear on the **sequence**: Context ? Interested Parties ? SCOPE. Scope cannot be defined in isolation or before context is understood.
* Scope is **not a static document** — it should be refined iteratively via PDCA as understanding of the organization and its AI grows.
* Scope defines which AI systems are governed, which functions and processes are in scope, and which lifecycle stages are covered.
* An auditor's question on scope: "How did you determine this is in scope?" The answer must trace to context and risk analysis, not to an arbitrary management decision.
* The scope statement should be a bounded, coherent statement — not a technical catalogue. It must be usable as a certification boundary.
* During the Day 3 activity, participants reviewed sample scope statements and found that vague language (e.g., "all AI systems") without boundary qualifiers was insufficient.

## Risk Linkage (from 50 Sample AI Risks)

* Risk 44 — Lack of documented AI governance framework: vague or missing scope is the first governance gap
* Risk 30 — Lack of defined AI system ownership: scope must name which systems are governed to enable ownership assignment

## Implementation Notes

* Draft scope statement structure: [Organization name] operates an AIMS covering [AI activities/systems/functions] within [organizational units/locations], excluding [stated exclusions with justification].
* Align scope to ISMS scope if one exists — look for opportunities to share boundaries where appropriate.
* Confirm scope is signed off by top management before policy and risk activities begin.
* Keep scope succinct enough to be communicated and understood — not a technical specification.
